    Courses

    Several organizations are working hard to give classrooms everywhere easy access to computer science education. Here are some popular courses and applications that your class can use to explore the adventurous world of programming.


    • Code.org

      For Educators

      Grades K-12

      Teachers say our five elementary courses are “totally awesome sauce” and fun for all students, even pre-readers. Our new middle school course can be offered as a semester or year-long introduction to computer science for all students. We offer two year-long courses for high school: an intro course and an AP course. Both are designed to broaden participation in computer science.

    • ScratchJr

      Massachusetts Institute of Technology

      Grades K-2

      ScratchJr is an introductory programming language that enables young children (ages 5-7) to create their own interactive stories and games. Children snap together graphical programming blocks to make characters move, jump, dance, and sing. Children can modify characters in the paint editor, add their own voices and sounds, even insert photos of themselves -- then use the programming blocks to make their characters come to life.

    • Tynker™

      Grades K-8

      The curriculum is organized into grade-specific courses where students move at their own pace through scaffolded lessons to learn concepts and apply them creatively. Tynker includes everything you need to teach programming in a fun, engaging way, including lesson plans, teacher guides, answer keys, and standards alignment.

    • Scratch

      Massachusetts Institute of Technology

      Grades 3-10

      Scratch is a programming language and an online community where children can program and share interactive media such as stories, games, and animation with people from all over the world. As children create with Scratch, they learn to think creatively, work collaboratively, and reason systematically. Scratch is designed and maintained by the Lifelong Kindergarten group at the MIT Media Lab.

    • CodeHS

      Grades 9-12

      CodeHS courses teach students applicable computer science skills. With a focus on helping students develop problem solving and computational thinking skills, students come away both with a knowledge of professional programming languages and the conceptual understanding needed to learn new languages.

    Frequently Asked Questions

    Here are some of the most common questions among new computer science teachers.


    What is computer programming? Is it different than coding?

    Computer programming is the process of writing instructions for a computer to follow in order to achieve a task. Generally, as seen in this site, the terms "programming" and "coding" are used synonymously. However, the term "programming" technically refers more to the creation of logical steps and algorithms, whereas "coding" refers to the simpler grunt work of creating structures and translating between languages. "Coder" and "coding" are simply playful, non-intimidating terms that are often used to attract new audiences to the field, but are not commonly used in professional settings. It's polite to refer to professional programmers as "programmers" or "developers."

    Why should we teach computer programming in schools?

    Children deserve to learn about the world around them - including the technology that has become so integral to modern life. Computer programming nurtures logic, problem-solving, and creativity, resulting in positive effects on growth in other subject areas. Did I mention students love computer science?

    My school already teaches online safety, typing, and the Office Suite. Is that enough? Do these count as computer science?

    These are great examples of "digital literacy," or the ability to interact with computers as an average user. Digital literacy is separate from computer science. Think of digital literacy as the ability to eat food, while computer science is the study of cooking. Computer science, like cooking, opens up a new world of possibilities for creation and improvements. Without cooking, you are forced to eat only what others have made.

    Can I teach computer programming even if I haven't coded pefore?

    Yes. This is a great opportunity to teach students that learning is a lifelong adventure - even teachers are learning! The key is to embrace the idea that teachers and students are learning together.

    Am I too old to learn coding?

    No! Coding is a skill that can be learned at any age with a little practice and patience. It's important that we set good examples for our students and don't easily give up when facing a challenge - and computer programming is no exception! Many adults find that they really enjoy programming and wish they had had the opportunity to learn it earlier in life.

    What if my students are too young? Will the syntax of coding be an issue?

    There are many activities, books, and games designed speficially for pre-reading children learning to code. Students of all ages are encouraged to develop computational thinking skills, or the ability to solve problems algorithmically.

    My students' coding projects have a lot of bugs. Does this mean there is a problem with my teaching?

    Not necessarily. Computers do exactly what we tell them to do, and as humans, we almost always produce imperfect code the first time. Unlike in most other academic subjects, perfection in programming is not a reasonable goal. Professional programmers have bugs all the time; bugs are expected during the development process and should not be a source of shame. This can be a great learning opportunity for students struggling with confidence and perfectionism. Learning to code forces students to persevere through small failures in order to achieve a higher goal. The key is learning to fail fast and move on.

    Are there other teachers in my area who can help me learn?

    Code.org has created an API for helping people find local coding schools. This is a great resource for finding other teachers in your community that are also learning and teaching computer programming. Additionally, the Computer Science Teachers Association sponsors events to unite computer science teachers all over the country.
